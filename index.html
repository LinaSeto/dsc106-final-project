Alarming Coral-ations: How Climate Change Impacts Life Under The Sea

The reef remembers
every degree.

Between 2002 and 2024, the Great Barrier Reef endured six mass bleaching events — more than in the previous century combined. The water it lives in warmed by less than two degrees. This is the story of that small number, and what it took with it.

Where the reefs are

Coral reefs cover less than one percent of the ocean floor, yet they shelter roughly a quarter of all marine species. They cluster in a narrow tropical band — warm enough to grow, cool enough to survive.

One reef, the size of a country

The Great Barrier Reef stretches 2,300 kilometres along Australia's northeast coast. From orbit, it is the largest living structure on Earth.

A baseline temperature

For most of the satellite era, sea surface temperature here held remarkably steady. Corals built their skeletons against a familiar rhythm of seasons.

The summer of 2016

Zoom in to one place, one summer. Each point is the 8-day mean sea surface temperature off Cairns, on the northern Great Barrier Reef. A whole year, one data point every week and a half.

A line corals cannot cross

Above the dashed line — roughly 1°C above the warmest month's historical average — corals begin to expel the algae living in their tissues. They turn ghost white. They are still alive, but starving.

Six weeks above the threshold

In February and March 2016, the water at Cairns spent more than six consecutive weeks above the bleaching threshold, peaking at 30.6°C. The reef did not recover.

And it happened again

Six years later, in 2022, summer started warm and stayed warm. December 2021 was already at the threshold. The peak wasn't as extreme as 2016, but the warmth lasted longer.

A pattern, not a one-off

Human Impact

Food Scarcity

Coral reefs feed an estimated 500 million people directly. After mass bleaching, reef fish biomass can drop by 60–80% within five years. For coastal fishing communities, especially in the Indo-Pacific, this loss is measured in empty nets and rising food prices.

Coastal Protection

Healthy reefs absorb up to 97% of wave energy before it reaches shore. Without them, coastal flooding damage roughly doubles during storms. For low-lying island nations and densely populated coasts, the reef is the only barrier between communities and the open ocean.

Ecosystem Impact

Habitat Loss

A quarter of all marine species spend part of their lives on coral reefs — using them as a nursery, a hunting ground, or a refuge. When the coral skeleton breaks down, the community above it disperses. Fish, octopuses, sea turtles, sharks — all lose the structure their lives depend on.

Ecosystem Impact

Food Web Collapse

Reef fish are prey for larger predators, which are prey for still larger ones. When the reef collapses, the loss cascades outward — to open-ocean species, seabirds, marine mammals, and eventually to commercial fisheries hundreds of kilometres away.

A pattern, not a place

A reef can survive one bleaching.
Sometimes two.

What's happened on the Great Barrier Reef is happening everywhere — the Caribbean, the Maldives, the Coral Triangle, the Red Sea. In 1998, the world saw its first global bleaching event. In 2010, the second. The fourth, declared in 2024, is still unfolding. The intervals between events are shrinking faster than corals can rebuild.

Corals grow slowly — a tabletop colony can take decades to mature. When mass bleaching arrives every few years, there is no longer time to recover between events.
